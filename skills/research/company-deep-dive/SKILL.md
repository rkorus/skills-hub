# Skill: Company Deep Dive

## What This Builds

A research brief that makes you the most prepared person in the room before a sales pitch, partnership discussion, or business engagement.

Not a summary of their website. Not industry boilerplate. Real intelligence:

- **Real company size/revenue/locations** — triangulated from permits, employee count, review velocity
- **Tech stack assessment** — what software they are actually running (or not), not what they claim
- **Top pain points with $ estimates** — specific to their scale, their market, their review patterns
- **Decision-maker profiles** — names, titles, tenure, how they think, what they care about
- **THE HOOK** — their own words about their pain, pulled from job postings, website copy, or review responses
- **Reputation data** — review counts, ratings, complaint patterns, response behavior

When you walk in with this brief, you are not selling. You are diagnosing. That is the difference.

---

## When to Use

- Before any enterprise sales call or pitch where close rate matters
- Before a first meeting with a potential client where you need instant credibility
- Before a partnership discussion where you need to understand their real leverage
- Before competitive analysis where you need to understand their actual market position
- Any time "I Googled them" is not enough

Time investment: 15-45 minutes depending on depth required and company complexity.

---

## The 4-Agent Parallel Research Model

Run these four agents in parallel. Each owns a distinct research lane. No duplication, no gaps.

### Agent A: General Deep Dive

**Mission**: Paint the full picture — who they are, what people say about them, how they present themselves, and most importantly, what they have said about their own pain.

**Sources to hit**:
- Company website (all pages, especially About, Team, Careers, blog posts)
- Google Maps listing (review count, rating, response patterns, Q&A)
- Yelp, Angi, HomeAdvisor, BBB (cross-platform reputation check)
- LinkedIn company page (employee count trend, recent posts, job activity)
- Facebook/Instagram (community engagement, complaint threads)
- Indeed and Glassdoor job postings (THIS IS GOLD — see The Holy Grail section)
- Recent news mentions, press releases
- Any podcast appearances, interviews, or public statements by leadership

**What to produce**:
- Company narrative (founding story, growth arc, current positioning)
- Review pattern analysis (what customers love, what they complain about, how leadership responds)
- Social proof signals (awards, certifications, association memberships)
- THE HOOK (their own words — mandatory output, see below)

**KILLER HOOK HUNT** — Agent A's most important job. Before writing anything else, scan:
1. Careers/Jobs page — what problems are they trying to hire their way out of?
2. About page — what do they say about their mission or growth?
3. Any blog posts or news — what challenges do they mention?
4. Review responses from leadership — what do they promise they are fixing?
5. Job posting titles — "Dispatcher Needed ASAP" tells you they have a scheduling crisis

---

### Agent B: Permit and Corporate Research

**Mission**: Get the hard facts — legal entity, officers, founding date, license status, permit history, real project volume.

**Sources to hit**:
- State contractor license lookup (Florida: myfloridalicense.com/DBPR; other states have equivalents)
- Secretary of State / corporate registry (Florida: search.sunbiz.org; search by company name and officer name)
- BuildZoom.com (permit aggregator — pull total permits pulled, permit types, geographic spread, recency)
- County permit portals if BuildZoom is incomplete (most Florida counties have public portals)
- USASpending.gov (government contracts — often reveals true scale for government-adjacent businesses)
- SAM.gov (federal contractor registrations)

**What to produce**:
- Legal entity name and structure (LLC vs Corp vs DBA)
- Officer names and titles (these are your actual decision-makers, not just LinkedIn)
- Founding date (years in business = how set-in-their-ways they are, or how hungry)
- License number and status (active/expired/disciplinary history)
- Total permit volume and types (reveals true project throughput)
- Geographic footprint from permit data (which counties they actually work in)
- Any disciplinary actions or complaints on record

---

### Agent C: Competitive Landscape

**Mission**: Understand where this company sits in their market — who they compete with, how they compare, and where the gaps are.

**Sources to hit**:
- Google "best [service] in [city]" — who shows up alongside them?
- Angi/HomeAdvisor pro listings for their category and geography
- Review comparison across competitors (volume, rating, recency, response rate)
- LinkedIn search for similar companies in their market
- BuildZoom rankings if applicable
- Any local business association membership lists

**What to produce**:
- Top 3-5 named competitors with basic profiles
- Review comparison table (company vs competitors: count, rating, recency)
- Differentiators: where does the target company win? Where do they lose?
- Market position: are they the dominant player, a challenger, or a niche operator?
- Gaps the competitor set reveals — pain points the whole category has, not just this company

---

### Agent D: Financial and Scale Signals

**Mission**: Triangulate revenue and true scale from proxy signals. No company is going to give you their P&L — you build it from what is publicly observable.

**Sources to hit**:
- LinkedIn employee count (and trend over time if visible)
- Indeed job postings (count of open roles — a proxy for growth vs contraction)
- Dun & Bradstreet public profile (revenue range, employee count estimates)
- ZoomInfo public signals (if accessible without a paid account)
- SBA loan database (reveals borrowing history, scale signals)
- Google Maps listing count per location (number of active locations)
- Fleet signals: do they have a Google Street View presence? Vehicle livery photos in reviews?
- Real estate footprint: do they own or lease their facility? (County property records)
- Vehicle registration volume if county data is accessible (fleet size = revenue proxy for field service)

**What to produce**:
- Revenue triangulation (see formula below) — show your work
- Employee count estimate (LinkedIn + job postings + tech-to-office ratio)
- Tech/field ratio (critical for FSM software sizing)
- Growth signal (hiring, new locations, new service lines = expanding; layoffs, review decline = contracting)

## Wave Structure

Use waves when the pitch is high-stakes or when Wave 1 reveals something unexpected that changes the research direction.

### Wave 1: Corporate Fundamentals (Agent B only)

Run Agent B first. This is the fastest wave — structured databases return clean data quickly.

**Why first**: Corporate and permit data grounds everything else. You need the legal entity name to search correctly on other platforms. You need the permit volume to anchor the revenue triangulation. You need the license status to confirm they are a real, active business.

**Time**: 5-10 minutes.

**Decision point after Wave 1**: Is this company bigger or smaller than expected? Does the officer list reveal decision-makers you did not know about? Does the permit history reveal geographic spread that changes the pitch angle? Adjust Agent A, C, and D priorities before launching Wave 2.

### Wave 2: Market Position (Agents A + C in parallel)

Launch Agent A and Agent C simultaneously. They do not depend on each other's output.

**Why parallel**: The qualitative picture (reviews, story, hook) and the competitive picture (market position, gaps) are independent research lanes. Running them simultaneously cuts time in half.

**Time**: 15-25 minutes for thorough coverage.

**Decision point after Wave 2**: You now have the hook, the competitive context, and the reputation picture. Is there a specific pain point that is emerging as dominant? Does the competitive gap point to something Agent D should specifically look for in the financial signals?

### Wave 3: Financial Triangulation (Agent D)

Agent D runs last because it synthesizes signals from Wave 1 (permit volume, entity structure) and Wave 2 (employee count from LinkedIn, review velocity from Agent A).

**Why last**: The revenue triangulation formula requires inputs from the other agents. Running D in isolation produces weaker estimates.

**Time**: 10-15 minutes.

## The Holy Grail

The single most valuable thing you can find in any company research session is something the company said about their own pain.

Not what a reviewer said. Not what a competitor said. What THEY said.

**Why it matters**: When you walk into a pitch and say "I noticed you wrote on your website that you can't keep up with the workload" — you are not selling anymore. You are reflecting their reality back to them. They already know the pain is real. You just showed them you did your homework.

**Where to find it**:

1. **Job postings** — This is the most reliable source. Every job posting is a company admitting a gap.
   - "Dispatcher needed immediately" = scheduling is breaking
   - "Experienced CSR to handle high call volume" = they are losing calls
   - "Office manager to implement processes" = they have no processes
   - "Service manager to supervise growing team" = they scaled past their management capacity
   - Read the job description body, not just the title. Companies often describe the problem they are hiring to solve.

2. **About page / Our Story copy** — Look for phrases like:
   - "Demand for our services has grown faster than expected"
   - "We have expanded to [X] locations"
   - "Our team has grown from [small number] to [large number]"
   - These are pride statements that also reveal the scale of the operational challenge underneath.

3. **Awards and recognition pages** — Companies that win "Top [X] in growth" awards often include quotes from leadership about what drove that growth. Those quotes often reveal the operational strain.

4. **Owner/GM responses to negative reviews** — When leadership responds to a negative review, they often promise improvement. That promise reveals what they are currently failing at.
   - "We are working on improving our scheduling system" = scheduling is a known problem
   - "We have hired additional staff to reduce wait times" = they are capacity-constrained
   - "Our office team is expanding" = the back office is a bottleneck

5. **Their own marketing copy** — Service companies often market against their own pain:
   - "We answer the phone when others don't" = their competitors (and maybe they) have phone coverage problems
   - "Same-day service available" = urgency and scheduling is a selling point because it is so rare

**The Meares example**: Their Diamond Club page included the phrase "Can't keep up with the workload." That is a company with 150+ technicians and 29,000 Google reviews telling you directly that demand is outpacing their capacity to fulfill. That one sentence is worth more than three hours of market research.

## Revenue Triangulation Formula

For service companies (trades, home services, field service), use all three methods and report a range with confidence level.

### Method 1: Permit Volume

```
(Total permits in trailing 12 months) x (average project value for their trade)
= Revenue floor estimate
```

Calibration notes:
- Plumbing repair: $300-800 average ticket
- Plumbing installation: $2,000-15,000 average ticket
- HVAC replacement: $5,000-15,000 average ticket
- Electrical service call: $200-600 average ticket
- Electrical panel/rewire: $2,500-10,000 average ticket

If you can split permit types, apply the right multiplier to each bucket.

### Method 2: Employee Count

```
(Total employee count) x (industry average revenue per employee)
= Revenue midpoint estimate
```

Calibration notes:
- Residential trades (plumbing, HVAC, electrical): $150K-350K revenue per field tech
- Mixed residential/commercial: $250K-500K per tech
- Primarily commercial: $400K-800K per tech
- Support staff (office, dispatch, management): typically 1 per 5-8 field techs

Get employee count from LinkedIn. Adjust for office-to-field ratio — a company with 150 total employees in trades likely has 100-120 field techs and 30-50 support staff.

### Method 3: Review Velocity

```
(Total Google reviews) / (years in business) = annual review rate
(Annual review rate) / (industry review rate %) = implied annual job volume
(Annual job volume) x (average ticket) = revenue estimate
```

Industry review rate benchmarks (percentage of customers who leave reviews):
- Residential trades: 2-8% of completed jobs
- Best-in-class reputation management programs: 10-20%

A company with 29,000 reviews over ~20 years = ~1,450 reviews/year. At a 5% review rate = ~29,000 jobs/year. At $500 average ticket = $14.5M implied. But if average ticket is $2,500 (installation-heavy) = $72.5M implied. This is why combining with Method 2 is essential.

### Reporting the Range

Always show your work. Never present a single number.

```
Method 1 (permit volume): $X - $Y
Method 2 (employee count): $X - $Y
Method 3 (review velocity): $X - $Y
Triangulated range: $X - $Y (high confidence / medium confidence / low confidence)
Confidence note: [what signals would move this estimate up or down]
```

## Anti-Patterns

**Never use generic industry stats as findings.**
"The plumbing industry averages 15% annual growth" is not research. It is filler. Every finding in the brief must be company-specific, sourced, and attributed.

**Do not stop at Wave 1 if the pitch matters.**
Wave 1 alone gives you corporate facts. It does not give you the hook, the competitive position, or the revenue picture. If you would be embarrassed to walk in with only corporate facts, run all three waves.

**Revenue estimates must show the triangulation method.**
Presenting "$50M revenue" without showing how you got there is a guess dressed up as research. Show Method 1, Method 2, and Method 3. Report a range. State your confidence level. The triangulation method is what makes the estimate credible.

**Job postings are often the most revealing source — check them first.**
Most researchers go to the website first. The website is curated marketing. Job postings are unfiltered operational reality. Before you read a single word of their About page, open Indeed and search their company name. The job postings tell you what is broken right now.

**Do not skip the review response analysis.**
How leadership responds to negative reviews is a direct window into company culture and operational awareness. A company that responds defensively to every negative review tells you something about how they will receive your pitch. A company that responds with "we are actively working on improving our scheduling" tells you they already know what you are about to tell them.

**Do not conflate LinkedIn employee count with field tech count.**
A plumbing company with 200 LinkedIn employees may have 120 field techs, 40 office staff, 20 apprentices, and 20 management. The revenue per field tech multiplier only applies to the tech count. Ask: what is the tech-to-office ratio for this type of company at this scale?

**Never present findings without THE HOOK.**
If you cannot find something the company said about their own pain, you have not looked hard enough. Every company leaks something. Look harder. The hook is not optional — it is the difference between a pitch and a conversation.
